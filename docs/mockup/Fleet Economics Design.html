Co-op autodelen · Design Doc · Fleet economics

More km. More drivers. Less burden.

A design for a payout flow where the co-op earns the owner's fixed costs back — one kilometre at a time.
Design

The old flow asked "did this year balance?" The new flow asks a better question: "How many kilometres of co-op driving does it take to lift the fixed costs off the owner's shoulders?" — and shows the answer live, every week of the year.

§ 1

Principles

Three rules the new system commits to. Every screen, every calculation follows from these.

Principle 1

Owner drives at cost, others drive at price.

When Roeland drives his own Jean-François, he pays only the variable cost (fuel + wear) — because he'd burn that fuel and incur that wear anyway. When anyone else drives, they pay the full co-op rate, which includes a contribution to fixed costs.

This is what makes the next two principles possible.

Principle 2

Every co-op km pays down the owner's fixed costs.

Insurance, tax, inspection, depreciation — these are real annual burdens on the owner. When another member drives a km, their fee is split: variable part covers fuel/wear, the rest goes into a fixed-cost recovery bucket for that car.

The owner can see that bucket fill up all year. At break-even, the bucket is full. Beyond break-even, the co-op is now paying the owner back — which is exactly the nice surprise that makes autodelen feel generous.

Principle 3

The payout is a story, not a spreadsheet.

At year-end, Malvina doesn't get "here are 14 rows of debits and credits." She gets a narrated statement: "Ethel drove 11.400 km this year. Others drove 6.800 of those. Their contributions covered about a third of your fixed costs — €870 out of €2.640. That's a real lift from the co-op. You carried the rest yourself, as expected for a car you own and use."

§ 1½

Owner vs. admin — two hats, usually the same head

Before the math, a quick word on who sees what. The app has two distinct responsibilities. Today they overlap completely in your co-op. The data model should not assume that will always be true.

Owner

Has skin in the game

Scope: one specific car.

Derived from: car.ownerIds[] — not a role flag.

Decides: km-rate for their car · accepts a repair quote · whether the car stays in the co-op.

Sees: break-even for their car, their payout, their fixed-cost recovery.

Admin

Runs the co-op

Scope: the whole co-op.

Derived from: member.isAdmin flag.

Decides: approves new members · closes the year · handles disputes · bulk data cleanup.

Sees: everyone's settlement, all cars, data-hygiene tools.

The model

Owner is data. Admin is a flag.

An owner is whoever appears in car.ownerIds[] — no role attribute needed. This lets us support co-owned cars (Roeland + Malvina on Jean-François) naturally, and makes "you are an owner" self-evident from the data.

member.isAdmin is a separate boolean. In your co-op today, everyone who runs settlements gets it — probably 3–4 people. A member can be admin without owning a car (e.g. a treasurer), or own a car without wanting admin burden.

One consequence: a single member can see both sets of screens. The UI should switch hats gracefully, not gate.

How the UI reflects this

  • Everyone sees the normal member app — trips, fuel, reservations, their yearly statement.
  • Anyone in any car.ownerIds also sees a "My cars" section — fleet tiles, break-even detail, payout — filtered to just their cars. Not a separate role, just a conditional section.
  • Admins see a "Beheer" tab on top of that — co-op-wide: all members, all cars, data hygiene, yearly close.
  • Co-owners (shared car) see the same My-cars screens with shared numbers. The payout splits by ownership share.
Implementation note: In the existing Beheer tab, rename the top-level to reflect this split. The current "Wagens" stub becomes My cars (visible to any owner) + All cars (admin-only). The current settlement screens stay under Beheer. This way a non-admin owner can still manage their rate without needing admin rights.

Authentication — the bit we don't have yet

Today the app picks a user from a dropdown. That was fine for the prototype; it is not fine the moment real money moves through it. We need proper sign-in before any of this ships.

Auth · v1

Email + password, nothing fancier

  • Sign up with email + password → lands in a pending state (no access yet).
  • An admin approves the pending account and links it to an existing member record (or creates a new one).
  • Password reset via email link. Magic-link as a nice-to-have later.
  • Sessions persist; logout is explicit and clears local state.

Why gated sign-up: autodelen is a closed co-op, not a public app. Admin approval is the membership gate.

Admin powers

Admins can promote (and demote) other admins

  • On the Leden beheren screen, each member row has a toggle: isAdmin.
  • Flipping it takes effect immediately; the affected member sees the Beheer tab on next load (or via a toast + live update).
  • Safety rail: you cannot demote the last admin. The UI greys out the toggle with an explainer.
  • Audit trail: each promotion/demotion is logged (who, when) — visible to all admins. Small but essential for trust.
  • Self-demote allowed — an admin can step down, as long as someone else remains admin.
Build order: Auth is a prerequisite for real use but not for iterating on the economics screens. Mock the auth layer now (session = pretend-logged-in member), design the Leden-beheren admin toggle as part of the UI work, and implement real auth as its own focused sprint before launch.
§ 2

The break-even math

Meet Malvina. She co-owns Ethel (a Fiat Punto). Looking at the 8-year numbers, Ethel has been running at roughly one-third cost recovery — the co-op has consistently covered about a third of her fixed costs, and she has carried the rest as her share of owning a car she also uses. That's a real contribution from her fellow members. But Malvina never really knew that: the old spreadsheet showed her owing or being owed a few hundred euros at year-end, not what fraction of her fixed costs the co-op was helping with. Let's walk through how the new system makes that visible:

— Ethel · yearly cost structure · Malvina —
Fixed costs (owner burden)
€ 2.640
Variable · per km
€ 0,12 / km
Co-op rate · per km
€ 0,23 / km
→ Contribution to fixed
€ 0,11 / km

Each co-op km contributes € 0,11 towards Ethel's €2.640 of fixed costs. In 2024, Ethel saw 6.800 co-op km from non-Malvina drivers — meaning the co-op covered roughly €870 of her €2.640 fixed costs, about a third. That's a real lift. Malvina carried the remaining two-thirds herself, which is the normal deal for a car she owns and uses. The new number the old system never showed her is simply: how much did the co-op help this year, in plain euros?

Break-even (where co-op km fully cover fixed costs) would be €2.640 ÷ €0,11 ≈ 24.000 co-op km — a stretch for a car Ethel's size. That's fine. Break-even is not a target Malvina must hit; it's a reference point so she can see where she stands. Landing at one-third or one-half cost recovery is a fair outcome the co-op should feel good about. And any year she goes past break-even, the surplus flows back to her as a payout.

— Malvina's out-of-pocket burden on Ethel as co-op km rise —

The chart is the hero of the new design. The red line going down to zero is the honest story: "every kilometre driven by another member reduces your burden by 11 cents." Beyond break-even, the line crosses into green.

§ 3

Screens

Three screens put this model in the owner's hand. All three share the same visual language as the existing app.

Screen 1 · My cars · Malvina sees Ethel's pace

This is where Malvina finally gets the answer she never had: is Ethel paying for herself? One tile per car she (co-)owns, each shows:

  • The burden meter — a line from "full fixed costs" to 0. Filled in as co-op km accumulate.
  • Current burden — the big number: what Malvina would still owe if the year ended today.
  • Projected landing — "at current pace, Ethel ends the year at −€1.310." Previously invisible.
  • vs. last year — a quiet compare strip that shows whether this year is trending better or worse.
  • Break-even gap — the 17.200 km Ethel still needs. A single number that answers "how serious is this?"

This turns the blind-guess era into a live barometer. Malvina no longer has to wait for December to find out she lost money.

Tiles are stamped green, amber, or red. Green: past break-even. Amber: projected below break-even but close. Red: significant gap — needs action.

Screen 2 · Break-even detail · Where Malvina's money went

Tap Ethel's tile → a full-screen detail. The hero is the break-even curve (same chart as §2). Below it, a plain-English narration of who helped and by how much:

  • Contribution ledger — each member's km contribution to Ethel's fixed-cost bucket, sorted by biggest helper first. Roeland shows up at the top because he drove Ethel a lot in 2024.
  • What-if slider — "if Ethel's rate were €0,42 instead of €0,23, you'd be at break-even right now." Honest about the trade-off.
  • Repair shock marker — any big repair that month is pinned on the curve, so Malvina sees cause-and-effect (e.g. remblokken in July cost €420).

This is the screen Malvina will actually open when she wants to understand — not just track.

Screen 3 · Rate assistant · The January decision

Every January, Malvina opens this for Ethel. It is not a calculator. It's a conversation. Three questions in plain language:

  • "How many co-op km do you realistically expect this year?" — with last 6 years pre-filled as tappable reference.
  • "How fast do you want fixed costs to be covered?" — a slider from "be generous to members" to "recover fully by year-end."
  • "Want a surplus for surprises?" — optional buffer for unexpected repairs.

Output: a suggested km-rate with the break-even km and projected burden. For Ethel, the system is honest: even at €0,27 there's a gap — because the real problem is low co-op usage, not the rate. Malvina commits with one tap and knows what she's signing up for.

No spreadsheet. No gut-feel. Just three honest choices.

§ 4

The yearly flow, reframed

From four events; each event has one clear question and one clear screen:

▸ January

Set the rate

"How fast should fixed costs be covered?" → Rate assistant (Screen 3).

▸ All year

Watch burden melt

"Is each car on track?" → Fleet tiles (Screen 1).

▸ Mid-year check

Adjust if needed

"Should I raise the rate after that big repair?" → Break-even detail (Screen 2) with "raise rate" action.

▸ December

Two statements

Member settlement (already exists) + Owner payout (new, narrated).

"Instead of a yearly moment of truth, a continuous moment of clarity."
§ 5

What to build

Concretely, in the existing app codebase:

Data

Extend the car model

  • Per car: fixed costs (insurance, tax, inspection, depreciation — already in the data model).
  • Per car: expected yearly co-op km (new · set in January).
  • Per car: variable cost per km (derived · fuel ÷ km + wear ÷ km from last year).
  • Derived: break-even km = fixed ÷ (rate − variable).
Screens

Three new screens / variants of Beheer

  • Fleet tiles on admin home (replaces the current "Wagens" stub).
  • Break-even detail per car (replaces the current km-price helper).
  • Rate assistant modal / screen for January decision.
  • Owner payout v2 — narrated, per-car, with "€X of your fixed costs were covered by others" as the hero number.
Principle

Every screen answers one question

The current app mixes member-settlement thinking and owner-accounting thinking. The redesign ruthlessly separates them. If an owner has a question about the fleet, they go to a fleet screen. If they have a question about a member, they go to a settlement screen. Never both.
