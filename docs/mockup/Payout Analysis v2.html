Others' contributions roughly covered the extra wear they caused. You were neither exploited nor subsidised by any large margin. The biggest single hit was 2022: Jean-Francois alone was −€2.209 from others because 82% of its km were driven by others during a year with big repair bills.

Over 8 years, others paying to use your cars covered about 94% of the extra costs they caused. Close to break-even. The "am I subsidising the co-op?" answer for both of you is "barely — maybe €200–300/year on average in marginal costs". That's very different from "losing €300+/year" implied by v1 of this document.

§ 4

The trap hidden in this math

Before celebrating, one honest note. The v2 analysis makes two simplifying moves, and it's worth naming them:

⚠ Assumption 1 · Fuel scales linearly with km

I split fuel cost by km-share. Fair for petrol: a car burns roughly the same L/100km regardless of driver. Small error possible (highway vs city driving).

⚠ Assumption 2 · Repairs scale linearly with km

This is the bigger assumption. A keuring happens yearly whether the car moves 2.000 km or 20.000. Insurance is flat. Only real wear costs (tires, brakes, timing belt) scale with km. So the "attributed exp to others" number is probably slightly over-attributed to them.

If I'd separated "flat" costs (keuring, verzekering, belasting) from "wear" costs (repairs, tires, brakes), the net-from-others number would have been better for the owners — probably +€1.000 to +€2.000 over 8 years instead of −€3.274. I'll wire this split into the redesign.

"Others chipping in roughly covered what they used. It didn't make you rich — but it didn't bleed you either."
§ 5

What this changes in the redesign

The v1 document proposed three shifts. Two still stand; one needs reframing:

Still correct · Shift 1

Split fixed vs variable costs explicitly.

Now with a sharper purpose: fixed costs (insurance, tax, keuring) stay with the owner and are not charged per-km to others. Only the wear-related variable costs feed into the km-price. This is the honest model — you'd pay the insurance with or without the co-op.

Still correct · Shift 2

Show the owner a running "net from others" tile.

Not a total-car-budget tile — an others-only tile. This matches how owners actually think: "the co-op part of this car, is it pulling its weight?"

Reframe · Shift 3

Don't scare owners with gross numbers.

The old "yearly statement" that showed revenue − all costs was making things look worse than they are, because owners were counting costs they'd have paid anyway. The new statement should have two clear numbers:

  • Net from co-op usage — what others' driving contributed or cost you, after their km-share of wear.
  • Your own usage, at cost price — what the co-op owes you or you owe it for your own trips (separate ledger).

Keep them separate. They answer different questions and mixing them is what made the old settlement confusing.

Revised TL;DR

The real answer to your 8-year question

The co-op was neither a quiet leak nor a windfall. Other members covered roughly what they used. You drove your own cars at your own expense — which you'd have done anyway. The feeling of "not knowing" was accurate: the old flow hid both the good news (€22k came in!) and the bad news (small marginal loss) behind one confusing settlement.

The redesign's job is to split these two stories and show each one honestly:

  1. What the co-op (others) did: revenue in − their fair share of wear-costs.
  2. What you did: your trips at cost price, your advanced fuel refunded.

With that split, you'll never again wonder whether the last year was a good year or a quiet disaster.
