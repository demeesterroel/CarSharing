▲ The big finding

Bessy is the villain. Jean-Francois has been profitable 6 out of 8 years. Ethel hovers near zero. But Bessy cost Roeland € 5.292 over 4 bad years (2022–2024) because €0,30/km simply doesn't cover an old car that needs repairs more than it gets driven. In 2024 Bessy cost € 1,41 per kilometre while you were charging € 0,30.

⚠ The pattern

Every year one car lands in a big repair hole — 2022 was catastrophic across all three. You set km-prices by gut feel at the start of a year based on last year's usage, but unexpected repairs are precisely what breaks the budget. Fuel is predictable; breakdowns are not.

§ 2

Why the current flow hides this

I went back through your process and identified four specific failures that caused you to not know the answer to your own question:

1

The owner's fixed costs never enter the math.

Your yearly dashboard settles between members — who paid fuel, who drove km, does that balance. But insurance, tax, inspection, and depreciation are paid by you and Malvina from your own wallets, and never show up as a debt the co-op owes you. So the question "did I break even?" is literally unanswerable in the current spreadsheet — the inputs aren't there.

This is the root cause

2

The km-price is guessed in January, judged in December.

You set € 0,30/km for Bessy based on a hunch about last year's usage. Then Bessy needed a € 1.250 keuring in October. You don't find out the price was wrong until you run the dashboard 3 months later — too late to fix that year, and the loss has already happened.

3

There is no moment in the year where the question "are costs covered?" is asked out loud.

The co-op operates on trust and casual tracking all year. Then, at settlement, everyone sees a number telling them what they owe each other. No one sees: "This year, Bessy earned € 578 and cost € 3.420. Roeland is carrying € 2.842 of that alone." The question is invisible, so the answer is invisible.

4

The output is addressed to members, not to owners.

Your current payout flow produces 12 rows of "member X pays Roeland € Y". Great for a well-functioning year; useless for diagnosing whether the underlying prices were right. There's no two-line summary that says "Roeland, your cars made or lost this much this year after everything."

"The system tells you who-owes-whom — but never tells the owner whether the whole enterprise is sustainable."
§ 3

The redesign — three shifts

The fix isn't one feature — it's three conceptual changes that need to land together.

Shift 1

Model fixed costs explicitly.

Every car gets a yearly fixed cost profile: insurance + tax + inspection + a chosen depreciation. This is entered once per year per car. It doesn't change how members pay (they still pay per km) — it changes how the co-op reimburses the owner.

Two new numbers now exist for every car:

  • Kostprijs per km = (yearly fixed + expected variable) ÷ expected km. This is the real break-even price.
  • Owner net = trip revenue − fuel − extra costs − fixed costs. This is what actually lands in the owner's account.

These two numbers are what answer your question.

Shift 2

Make the question visible continuously, not at year-end.

The co-op admin dashboard should always show, for each car, a running "year-to-date owner net" tile. Red stamp if losing money, green if covering costs. No new inputs required — just a running sum of data already being captured.

This turns a once-a-year panic moment ("oh, the keuring broke Bessy") into a rolling signal. If in August Bessy is already at − € 1.500, you can either:

  • raise the km-price mid-year (with clear narrative: "Bessy had a € 1.250 repair, price goes from € 0,30 → € 0,38"),
  • or consciously accept the loss and not be surprised.

Either way, you know.

Shift 3

The yearly settlement becomes two separate conversations.

Right now it's one confusing conversation ("let's close the books"). Split it:

Act 1

Member settlement

Each member pays for their trips, reclaims fuel / costs they advanced. Strictly member ↔ co-op fund. Ends with the fund holding a pot of money.

Act 2

Owner payout

The fund pays each owner: trip revenue − unreimbursed fuel/costs − the fixed costs they carried. The surplus/deficit is the answer to your question.

Because these are now distinct events, each one has a clear success criterion. Members stop arguing about insurance they never see; owners see whether the prices they set were right.

§ 4

What the new yearly flow looks like

Four events across the year, each with one simple question it answers:

▸

January — Price setting (with assistant)

For each car, the admin opens the km-rate assistant. It shows last year's actual costs, breaks them down per km, adds fixed costs, suggests a cost-covering price. Owner can override — but now override is a conscious choice with a visible shortfall number, not a guess.

Answer: "If usage stays similar, will this price cover costs? Yes / no, and by how much."

▸

All year — Rolling owner-net tile

Always visible on admin dashboard. A tile per car: current revenue minus current costs minus pro-rated fixed costs. Red stamp if off-track.

Answer: "Right now, is each car on or off track?"

▸

December — Member settlement

The existing dashboard, unchanged in spirit: every member gets one clear row — pay this, or receive this.

Answer: "What does each member owe, and what do they get back?"

▸

December — Owner payout

A new statement, one card per car. Revenue on top. Fuel, costs, fixed costs subtracted. One bottom number: net to owner. Plus a one-line explanation: "Bessy was profitable this year because usage went up and no repairs happened" or "Ethel lost € 600 because of an unexpected gearbox".

Answer: Exactly the question you've been asking for 8 years.

TL;DR

What changes, practically

  1. Add a "fixed costs per car per year" input (4 fields · 2 cars now, 3 next year · ~3 minutes).
  2. Compute and show owner net as a first-class number on the dashboard — not buried in the settlement.
  3. Split settlement into two cards: member pay-up, owner pay-out. Stop mixing them.
  4. Show the km-price assistant every January so pricing is never a blind guess again.

None of this requires a new behaviour from members. They still log trips, advance fuel, pay at year-end. The change is entirely in how the data you already collect is surfaced, and in the addition of fixed costs.
